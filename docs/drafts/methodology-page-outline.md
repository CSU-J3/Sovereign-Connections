# Methodology Page Outline — Sovereign Connections Tracker

**Working title:** "What this tracker counts, and why" (placeholder; final headline TBD)
**Target length:** ~3,500 words
**Surfaces:** Substack post (das_DEMARC) and `docs/methodology.html` (GitHub Pages) — single source written once, deployed both places
**Audience:** Oversight-literate non-specialists. Government ethics scholars, accountability reporters, congressional staff, civic-minded readers already following Lawfare / CREW / POGO. Sober, citation-forward, no causal claims.

## Section 1 — Opening (≈300 words)

**Lead:** A single concrete fact — the Tahnoon-Aryam transaction with WLF, $187M to Trump family entities four days before inauguration, undisclosed for over a year — and the question it forces. *Then* the framing: this is the tracker the central rule is built around, and this post is the rule before it gets applied.

**What the post does:** establishes the inclusion/exclusion rule, walks the boundary cases that test it, distinguishes it from adjacent frameworks, and explains what the rule deliberately does not capture.

**What the post does not do:** allege quid pro quo, speculate about intent, or claim the documented flows caused any policy outcome. The descriptive-not-prescriptive posture is the post's first commitment to the reader.

**Voice note:** open on the Tahnoon fact rather than a sweeping context paragraph. Don't open with "In an era of..." or any version of that.

## Section 2 — The core rule, v1.x baseline (≈500 words)

The rule has three defined terms doing the work. Refer to the existing PROJECT.md Defined Terms section for canonical text; the methodology page quotes that section directly rather than restating, to keep the rule and its public articulation in lockstep. Summary of what each term does:

- **Foreign sovereign and sovereign-adjacent money.** Direct payments from foreign government treasuries; sovereign wealth funds (SWFI-listed); state-owned enterprises; controlled subsidiaries of any of the above; and entities where a controlling-interest chain runs to a sovereign body. Public-market minority shareholdings without governance rights are not in. The Apollo case (Section 4) is the worked example for that boundary.

- **Named family member of the current administration.** Per PROJECT.md, an exhaustive list keyed to specific relationships (children and their spouses, siblings and their spouses, plus a Senate-confirmed-relative cascade clause). Borderline cases default to exclusion. Excludes friends, business associates without family tie, in-laws of in-laws, romantic partners short of marriage. The bright line is documented relationship, not proximity.

- **Financial interest.** Per 5 CFR 2640.103(a) and 18 USC 208(a). Five categories: ownership stake, employment by the entity, debt instruments held where the entity is obligor, beneficial interest in a trust where the entity is among the holdings, spousal interests imputed under 18 USC 208(a)(2). Anything outside these five categories doesn't count, even if it appears suspicious.

- **Connected business.** A separately defined term: an entity in which a named family member holds a financial interest (per the five categories), where the connection is established through OGE 278 filings, SEC filings, public corporate registrations, court filings, or foreign jurisdiction equivalents. News reporting alone does not establish a connection.

**Why this rule and not a broader one.** The rule is built around the foreign Emoluments Clause's central concern (foreign-state-source value reaching domestic officeholders) and around what's documentable from primary records. A broader rule captures more cases but drifts toward causal claims the documented record doesn't support.

**Why this rule and not a narrower one.** The Tahnoon-Aryam transaction would arguably fail a literal Emoluments Clause analysis (Trump family, not Trump personally; private vehicle, not foreign state proper). The tracker captures it because the controlling-interest chain runs through Tahnoon's official UAE roles to the cap table. Narrower-than-this misses the mechanism doing the work.

**Voice note for this section:** quote the actual PROJECT.md text where readers benefit from precision, paraphrase elsewhere. Avoid restating the definitions in the methodology page itself; the methodology page is a derivative document.

## Section 3 — Burisma: the case the rule excludes (≈450 words)

This section does the symmetry work. Lead with the case that fails the rule despite political salience.

**The fact pattern in brief.** Hunter Biden's board seat at Burisma Holdings, 2014–2019. Approximately $3.4 million flowed to a Rosemont Seneca Bohai entity per Reuters bank-record reporting. The Ukrainian gas company employed Hunter Biden during a period when his father was the U.S. point person on Ukraine policy. The case dominated 2019–2024 oversight discourse and remains a touchstone in current discourse.

**Why it fails the rule.** Burisma was a privately held Cyprus-incorporated holding company owned by Mykola Zlochevsky through Brociti Investments. No Ukrainian government equity at any level. No documented payments from the Ukrainian Treasury, any Ukrainian ministry, or any state-owned enterprise. The flows ran *from* a private company *to* board members, not *from* a sovereign source. The Burisma working reference walks each criterion in detail; this section summarizes.

**Why this matters for the methodology.** The rule the tracker uses is the rule that excludes Burisma. Stating that explicitly does two things: it shows the rule isn't tuned to a particular party, and it pre-empts the complaint that the project is selective. A Republican-side analogue with the same structural features (son of a senior official on the board of a foreign-oligarch-owned private company with revenues dependent on home-country state licensing) would also fall outside scope, by the same rule applied the same way.

**Voice note:** keep this section tight. It's load-bearing for the symmetry test, but the reader who already knows the Burisma file shouldn't have to re-read it. Lead with the fact pattern, walk the test, name the exclusion, point to the reference file for depth.

## Section 4 — The Apollo bound: the rule excluding even where intent is documented (≈500 words)

This section does work the secondary commentariat doesn't always do — it shows the rule excluding flows where there's documented strategic intent on the sovereign side. This is the harder boundary case.

**The fact pattern.** The Brook (52-story Brooklyn rental tower) financing came from Apollo Commercial Real Estate Finance Inc. (NYSE: ARI), a publicly traded mortgage REIT in which QIA holds under 10% common-and-preferred equity since 2015 with no board seat or approval rights. The Belgrove Palm Beach $100M loan came from Apollo Global Management itself per the NYT's October 5, 2025 correction.

**Why it fails the rule.** Both flows fail the controlling-interest-chain test. A minority public-market shareholder with no governance rights is not a sovereign control chain over a NYSE-listed REIT's lending decisions. A NYSE-listed asset manager's corporate balance sheet is not a sovereign-source vehicle. The NYT itself retracted the "Apollo trust, partly owned by Qatar" framing in its October 5 correction; the corrected language is in the Witkoff reference and quoted directly in this section.

**Why this is the harder case.** The 2017–2018 Allaham material is on the record. A former Qatar-government lobbyist documents two meetings — late 2017 at the St. Regis with Sheikh Mohammed bin Hamad al-Thani (Qatar emir's brother) discussing Park Lane and Trump access; early 2018 at QIA's Manhattan headquarters proposing investment in Trump-allied projects via Apollo's REIT. Allaham's "we needed access to his friends" line is on the record. The strategic intent on the sovereign side is documented.

**The rule excludes the flows anyway.** Intent without controlling-interest chain doesn't make a public-market minority shareholding into sovereign control. The methodology rule turns on structural mechanism, not articulated purpose. This is the post's clearest statement of the rule's character: it asks what happened, not why someone wanted it to happen.

**Symmetry note inline.** A Democratic-side analogue with the same structure — public-market minority shareholding, no governance rights, even with documented strategic intent — would fall out of scope by the same rule.

## Section 5 — Witkoff WLF: the case the rule includes under v1.x, with the convergent-interest flag (≈500 words)

The clean inclusion case. Lead with what the rule captures.

**The fact pattern.** January 16, 2025, four days before inauguration: Aryam (a vehicle controlled by Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al Nahyan — UAE National Security Advisor, deputy ruler of Abu Dhabi, chair of state-backed investment vehicles) acquired 49% of World Liberty Financial for $500 million. $187 million flowed to Trump family entities at signing; $31 million flowed to Witkoff-associated entities. The transaction was undisclosed for over a year. May 2025: MGX (Abu Dhabi state-backed, also chaired by Tahnoon) used WLF's USD1 stablecoin to settle a $2 billion investment in Binance. Trump family entities receive 75% of net WLFI token-sale proceeds and a cut of stablecoin profits per WLF corporate documentation.

**Why it qualifies under v1.x.** Trump children are co-founders. Trump family financial interest is documented in dollar terms at the Tahnoon transaction closing. Tahnoon's controlling-interest chain to UAE sovereign vehicles satisfies the sovereign-source test through his official roles plus state-backed firm chair status. MGX as Abu Dhabi state-backed is sovereign per SWFI methodology. Both are tracker records.

**The convergent-interest flag.** The same Tahnoon transaction sent value simultaneously to Trump family entities ($187M) and to Witkoff-associated entities ($31M). The flag attaches because Witkoff is a senior administration appointee (Special Envoy for Peace Missions) with a documented financial interest in the same business receiving the same sovereign-source flow as the principal's family. The flag is a record-level field, not an inclusion trigger; it places this record in a higher evidence category.

**What this section does not say.** It does not allege the transaction was a quid pro quo, that the AI chip export approvals to UAE-linked companies in November 2025 were caused by the WLF deal, or that Witkoff's negotiating posture in Iran or Gaza talks was influenced by his retained interests. The post documents flows and fields. The reader draws inferences.

## Section 6 — The v2.0 expansion: covered intermediaries (≈400 words)

The expansion bounded by portfolio overlap.

**The structural gap v1.x missed.** Senior appointees and designated envoys can hold retained financial interests in businesses receiving sovereign-source money from governments whose policy portfolios overlap with the appointee's official duties. The named-family-member rule misses these. The Witkoff Group / Pakistan-Roosevelt Hotel sequence is the worked example: Steve Witkoff personally negotiated the US-Pakistan exploration of redeveloping the Roosevelt Hotel in Manhattan (owned by Pakistan International Airlines Investment Limited, a Pakistani SOE) in January 2026, while retaining documented Witkoff Group financial interests per his August 2025 OGE Form 278e.

**The expansion.** A record qualifies under v2.0 where a senior administration appointee or designated envoy holds a documented financial interest in a business receiving foreign sovereign or sovereign-adjacent money from a government whose policy portfolio overlaps with the appointee's official duties. All four conditions, documented per record.

**The bound that prevents sprawl.** Portfolio overlap is the bounding mechanism. A Treasury appointee with retained interests in a Gulf-funded business does not qualify unless Treasury portfolio touches Gulf sovereign-financial relations. A Middle East envoy with the same retained interest does qualify. The test is documented per record, not assumed.

**What it includes about the appointee category.** Cabinet officers, sub-Cabinet at deputy/under-secretary or above, White House staff at Assistant-to-the-President or above, presidential envoys / special envoys / ambassadors-at-large. Not career officials. Not outside advisors without formal appointment. The line is contestable but defensible; it's drawn explicitly so disagreements about it can happen on the record.

**Versioning posture.** v1.x records logged before v2.0 adoption are not re-examined; the change is forward-looking. The CHANGELOG.md entry documents the diff, the rationale, and the worked examples that motivated the change. Every methodology change is versioned and dated.

## Section 7 — What this rule isn't (≈500 words)

This section is where readers stop conflating the tracker with adjacent frameworks. Probably the most useful 500 words in the whole post for the oversight-literate reader.

**Not the foreign Emoluments Clause analysis itself.** Constitutional Emoluments analysis turns on whether *the officeholder personally* receives the value. The tracker captures family-and-business receipts as a related-but-distinct category. A foreign Emoluments Clause case might or might not be brought on a record the tracker logs; the tracker documents the flow, not the constitutional theory. Reference the relevant case law (DC and Maryland v. Trump; Blumenthal v. Trump; the early-republic ratifying-era discussion) without rehashing it.

**Not 7031(c).** Section 7031(c) of the State Department appropriations act bars foreign government officials and their immediate family members from US entry where the Secretary has credible information of "significant corruption" or "gross violation of human rights." The statute lists "corruption related to the extraction of natural resources" as illustrative, language that makes 7031(c) look closer to this tracker's beat than it is. 7031(c) targets *foreign officials* for *their* conduct. The tracker tracks *domestic officeholders* receiving *foreign-state-source value*. Both interest themselves in foreign corruption; they operate on different transactions. Kolomoyskyy was publicly designated under 7031(c) in March 2021 for his conduct as Dnipropetrovsk Oblast Governor; that designation says nothing about whether Burisma payments to Hunter Biden were sovereign-source (they weren't).

**Not FARA.** FARA covers unregistered representation of foreign principals. A Trump-family-connected business receiving Gulf sovereign money is not a FARA question for the family member unless they are also acting as the foreign government's agent. The Allaham 2017 lobbying registration is a FARA record; the WLF Tahnoon transaction is not.

**Not FIRRMA or CFIUS jurisdiction.** FIRRMA gives CFIUS authority to review foreign acquisitions of US businesses on national-security grounds. The tracker documents flows; CFIUS reviews acquisitions for national-security risk. Some records overlap (a sovereign-fund acquisition triggering CFIUS review while also fitting the tracker's rule), but the analyses are separate.

**Not 18 USC 208 or 5 CFR Part 2640.** These cover the criminal and administrative conflict-of-interest framework for officeholders. They use the same five-category financial-interest definition the tracker uses (because the tracker borrows from them) but ask a different question: whether the officeholder is barred from participating in particular matters. Records may surface 208 exposure; the methodology page doesn't make 208 findings.

**Not 5 CFR 2635.204.** This regulation governs gifts from foreign governments to executive-branch employees, with thresholds and reporting requirements. Sovereign-source flows to *family members* and *connected businesses* sit outside the regulation's direct reach but are sometimes adjacent. Worth noting; not the tracker's frame.

**Not the Lobbying Disclosure Act.** LDA covers domestic lobbying expenditures. Different transaction class entirely.

**Cross-framework note.** Some records overlap with FARA filings, 7031(c) designations, FIRRMA/CFIUS reviews, 18 USC 208 issues, or Emoluments Clause litigation. When they do, the methodology page links to the adjacent record but does not assume the adjacent framework's verdict.

**Per-SWF appendix.** PROJECT.md commits the methodology page to a per-SWF governance appendix that grows with the registry. Each entry documents the SWF's governance structure, its relationship to its government, and the documentary basis for treating it as sovereign-source. The appendix is a separate page (`docs/swf-registry.html`) cross-linked from the methodology page; this section flags its existence and points readers there.

## Section 8 — Disclosed gaps and cadence (≈250 words)

How the project handles incomplete records and what readers should expect from updates.

**Disclosed-gap principle.** When a record's sovereign-source chain or financial interest can't be established from primary records, the gap is disclosed rather than papered over. The Apollo question is the live worked example: secondary reporting framed it one way, primary-record verification (and the NYT's own October 5 correction) closed it the other way. Disclosing gaps makes the records that survive verification stronger.

**Soft-flag principle.** Records that belong in the dataset but should not count toward headline totals (for instance, records where the sovereign-source finding is robust but the financial-interest documentation is weaker, or vice versa) carry a flag with a documented reason. The reason is on the record. The flag is not a hedge; it's a signal that the record exists at a different evidence level.

**Cadence.** This tracker has the slowest cadence of the three portfolio projects. Foreign sovereign flows surface through SEC filings, OGE Form 278e disclosures, FARA filings, court records (CFIUS, civil litigation), and reporting — each on its own clock. New records get logged when the underlying primary record posts; the tracker does not chase real-time cycles.

**Errors and corrections.** Errors get corrected publicly through CHANGELOG.md entries, not silent edits. The same standard the methodology applies to its sources applies to itself.

## Drafting notes for me, when we go from outline to draft

**Voice constraints.** No em-dashes (PROJECT.md house rule, full stop). Use commas, parens, colons, or sentence breaks. No banned words from the user preferences (no "delve," "underscore," "leverage," "robust," etc.). No "It's not just X — it's Y" structures. No restating-the-question openings. No grand-statement openings or wrap-up closings. PROJECT.md red-flag terms also banned: "paradigm shift," "unprecedented," "shocking," "staggering," "alarming." No rhetorical questions as closers. No manufactured "three takeaways" structure. No Watergate or Iran-Contra comparisons unless directly load-bearing to the foreign-influence mechanism argument. No geopolitical framing imports (Saudi human rights, UAE regional ambitions, Chinese strategic interests) that distract from the financial-flow claim. No "corruption" as load-bearing without citation to a specific legal definition. Plain prose, vary sentence length, drop preamble.

**Risk to watch for.** The methodology page is the post most likely to drift toward causal/intent claims because the reader will want them. Hold the line. Every concrete case in the post should make the structural finding and stop. The Tahnoon transaction sent $187M to Trump family entities at the same moment $31M went to Witkoff entities; that is the documented flow. What it caused is a separate question for a separate post.

**Citation discipline.** Every dollar figure cited needs a footnote pointing to the primary record (WSJ-reviewed deal documents for the Tahnoon allocations, OGE Form 278e for Witkoff retained interests, NYT October 5 correction for the Apollo Belgrove framing). No quoting more than ~15 words from any source; paraphrase by default.

**Structural choice to flag.** The Apollo section (Section 4) is unusual for a methodology page in that it walks a case the rule excludes despite documented intent on the sovereign side. The standard playbook would skip this and only include the inclusion cases. I'd argue including it is the post's strongest move, because it shows the rule doing the harder work — the work that protects the project from scope drift even when the case is publicly contested. If you want to cut a section for length, this is the one I'd most strongly argue against cutting.

**Length flex.** Total ~3,500 words against the targets above. Section 4 (Apollo, 500 words) is the section most likely to balloon during drafting because the material is rich; if it crosses 700 words during drafting, that's the signal to cut it down rather than expand the rest of the post to match.
