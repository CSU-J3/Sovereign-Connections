# Burisma: Working Reference for the Sovereign-Adjacent Methodology Test

This file pulls out what bears on the central methodology question — does Burisma meet the foreign sovereign or sovereign-adjacent definition this tracker uses — and disclosed gaps where primary records would need to be consulted before publishing on the case. Not a comprehensive history of the affair. Functional reference.

## Ownership and corporate structure

Burisma Holdings Limited was a Cyprus-registered holding company for a group of Ukrainian gas exploration and production subsidiaries. Founded 2002 by Mykola Zlochevsky and Mykola Lisin, each holding 50%. Lisin died in a 2011 car crash. From 2011 forward, control sat with Brociti Investments Limited (also Cyprus-registered), which is owned by Zlochevsky.

The company was formally dissolved on February 16, 2023, per Grokipedia citing Cyprus registry records. Operations continued through Ukrainian subsidiaries.

Subsidiaries while operating included Esko-Pivnich, Pari, Persha Ukrainska Naftogazova Kompaniya, Naftogaz Garant, KUB-Gas, and Astroinvest-Ukraine. In 2016, Burisma was Ukraine's second-largest privately owned natural gas producer after DTEK, accounting for 26% of all gas produced by privately owned companies and roughly 5% of total Ukrainian gas production. Production reached 1.3 billion cubic metres in 2018.

A 2012 study by the Anti-Corruption Action Centre (AntAC), a Ukrainian civil society organization, claimed the true beneficial owner was oligarch Ihor Kolomoyskyy operating through Privat Group, with Zlochevsky and others as front men. Kolomoyskyy has denied involvement. The journalist John Helmer has written extensively arguing for the Kolomoyskyy-as-true-owner thesis. UK Serious Fraud Office records accepted Zlochevsky as the documented owner.

On March 5, 2021, Secretary of State Antony Blinken publicly designated Kolomoyskyy under Section 7031(c) of the Department of State, Foreign Operations, and Related Programs Appropriations Act for "involvement in significant corruption." The State Department press release ties the designation to his 2014-2015 tenure as Governor of Dnipropetrovsk Oblast and cites his use of "political influence and official power for his personal benefit." His wife Iryna Kolomoyska, daughter Angelika Kolomoyska, and son Israel Zvi Kolomoyskyy were designated alongside him. The designation does not reference Burisma. It does not turn on the AntAC ownership thesis. It is grounded entirely in conduct as a sub-national official.

**Methodology read on ownership:** This is a private company. No Ukrainian state ownership of Burisma, Brociti, or any subsidiary in any documented record. No state controlling-interest chain. The company's ownership structure does not satisfy the sovereign or sovereign-adjacent definition.

## The state ties that exist

There are real state ties in this case. They run through the owner's history, not through ownership structure.

**Zlochevsky's ministerial tenure.** Zlochevsky served as Ukraine's Minister of Ecology and Natural Resources from July 2010 to April 2012, then as Deputy Secretary of the National Security and Defense Council until February 2014. The ministerial portfolio included issuing subsoil-use permits — the licenses Burisma's revenue depended on.

**Licenses issued during his tenure.** During Zlochevsky's 2010-2012 ministerial period, Burisma secured nine production licenses for hydrocarbon extraction. AntAC's research, cited widely in subsequent reporting, framed this as self-dealing. Ukrainian authorities later opened criminal investigations into whether the licenses were lawfully issued. NPR's October 2019 reporting quoted Concorde Capital's Alexander Paraschiy noting the timing.

**Yanukovych alignment.** Zlochevsky was a Yanukovych ally. He fled Ukraine when Yanukovych fled in February 2014. The Ukrainian Prosecutor General opened investigations and put him on a watch list.

**UK Serious Fraud Office case.** In April 2014, the UK SFO froze approximately $23 million in Zlochevsky-linked assets pursuant to a money-laundering investigation. The case was dropped in 2018 for insufficient evidence, with the SFO citing lack of cooperation from Ukrainian prosecutors.

**Methodology read on state ties:** The state ties documented here describe a private actor monetizing prior state office. That is a recognizable category in PEP-risk and post-office-corruption frameworks. It is not the category the foreign Emoluments Clause, FARA, or this tracker's sovereign-adjacent definition is designed to capture. The flow of money in the Burisma case runs *from* the privately-held company *to* board members. It does not run *from* a foreign sovereign treasury, sovereign wealth fund, state-owned enterprise, or controlling state interest. The license-issuance question concerns Ukrainian state regulatory conduct toward a private company, not foreign sovereign payment to a connected business.

## The state ties that don't exist

To be explicit about what the documented record does and does not show:

- No Ukrainian government equity stake in Burisma, Brociti, or any subsidiary.
- No documented payments from the Ukrainian Treasury, any Ukrainian ministry, or any Ukrainian state-owned enterprise to Burisma during Hunter Biden's board tenure.
- No documented controlling-interest chain leading to a Ukrainian state body.
- The 2014 cooperation agreement Burisma signed with KazMunayGas (Kazakhstan's national oil and gas company) is the closest documented contact between Burisma and a foreign SOE, but it post-dates Zlochevsky's ministerial tenure and is a commercial cooperation between operating companies, not a sovereign payment.

## Hunter Biden engagement

**Timeline.** Devon Archer joined the Burisma board April 22, 2014, one day after Vice President Biden's official trip to Ukraine concluded. Hunter Biden's appointment was announced May 12, 2014. House Ways and Means Committee documents reference an April 17, 2014 Burisma executive email to Archer about reaching "a reasonable balance" on Hunter Biden's role. Hunter Biden's term expired April 2019.

**Compensation.** Reuters reviewed bank records showing Burisma sent approximately $3.4 million between April 2014 and November 2015 to Rosemont Seneca Bohai LLC, an entity associated with Hunter Biden's business partners. The records show two monthly $83,333 payments labeled "consulting services." Reuters noted it could not independently verify the documents or how much Hunter Biden personally received. The Senate HSGAC/Finance joint report (September 2020) characterized payments as "as much as $50,000 per month" to Hunter Biden, citing news reporting rather than direct bank records. Estimates in circulation range from $50,000/month (Senate report) through $83,333/month (Reuters bank records) to higher figures (House Oversight characterizations of total board compensation). The variance reflects whether the figure describes Hunter Biden's personal take, the entity-level inflow, or the combined Archer/Biden board compensation.

**Other board members.** Aleksander Kwasniewski (former president of Poland) joined the board contemporaneously with Hunter Biden in 2014. Cofer Black (former CIA Counterterrorist Center director, foreign policy adviser to the Romney 2012 campaign) joined in 2017. The reputational-cleanup framing of these appointments was articulated publicly by AntAC's Daria Kaleniuk in 2019 NPR reporting.

## Investigations

The record of investigations into Burisma and Zlochevsky is long and largely inconclusive:

- Ukrainian Prosecutor General investigations (multiple) into license issuance, tax evasion, and money laundering. Resolved through tax payments totalling approximately 180 million hryvnias (roughly $7.4 million USD at then-rates), per Wikipedia citing Ukrainian sources. No high-level convictions.
- UK Serious Fraud Office investigation (2014-2018). Dropped for insufficient evidence.
- National Anti-Corruption Bureau of Ukraine (NABU) involvement in the Zlochevsky file (multiple investigations across years). No high-level convictions.
- US prosecutors' Hunter Biden tax investigation in Delaware addressed Burisma board income reporting questions but did not develop a Burisma-specific corruption case against Hunter Biden.

The October 2019 NPR reporting quoted Ukrainian prosecutors stating "no indication that Hunter Biden did anything wrong" with respect to Ukrainian law during his board tenure.

## The Shokin question

The Shokin question dominates public coverage of the case but is methodologically peripheral to this tracker's beat. Briefly:

In December 2015, then-Vice President Biden, in his role coordinating US-Ukraine policy, conditioned a $1 billion US loan guarantee on Ukraine's removal of Prosecutor General Viktor Shokin. Biden has stated publicly (Council on Foreign Relations, January 2018) that he made this demand. Shokin was removed in March 2016. Trump's 2019 framing held that Biden engineered Shokin's removal to halt a Burisma investigation. The contemporary record shows Western governments (US, EU, IMF) and Ukrainian anti-corruption activists shared the position that Shokin was *insufficiently* aggressive on corruption cases generally, including Burisma. NPR's reporting quoted Kaleniuk and other anti-corruption advocates supporting Shokin's removal at the time.

This is a quid-pro-quo / policy-influence question, not a sovereign-payment-to-connected-business question. It belongs in a different oversight beat. This tracker's "payment occurred" to "payment caused policy outcome" drift caution applies directly: even if you held that Burisma payments influenced Biden family policy positions on Ukraine, that's the harder-to-prove-from-public-records question the methodology page is meant to keep separate from the easier-to-document "this sovereign source paid this connected business" question.

## Methodology assessment

Walking the sovereign-and-sovereign-adjacent definition from PROJECT.md against the Burisma record:

| Criterion | Burisma |
|---|---|
| Direct foreign government payment | No |
| Sovereign wealth fund per SWFI list | No |
| State-owned enterprise | No (private; SWFI lists none of Burisma's subsidiaries as SOE) |
| Controlling-interest chain to sovereign | No (chain runs Brociti → Zlochevsky, both private) |
| Foreign government official acting in official capacity | No (Zlochevsky was former, not current; payments came from private company) |

**Burisma does not meet the sovereign or sovereign-adjacent definition.** The case falls outside this tracker's scope. The reasons are documented and the conclusion is symmetrical: a Trump-family-connected board seat at a foreign-oligarch-owned private company with revenues dependent on home-country state licensing would also fall outside scope, by the same rule applied the same way.

The right framing for the methodology piece: Burisma is the case the framework excludes despite political salience, and the rule that excludes it is the same rule that would exclude its Republican-side analogue. That symmetry is the point of stating the rule before applying it.

This doesn't mean the Burisma case raises no oversight concerns. It means the concerns it raises (post-office monetization by a former minister, board-seat influence-peddling by US-connected individuals, FARA exposure for unregistered foreign-influence work) belong in different oversight beats with different evidence rules. The Lobbying Disclosure Act and FARA questions about the Hunter Biden board engagement are real. They aren't *this tracker's* questions.

A note on the 7031(c) framework, which the methodology piece should distinguish from the sovereign-adjacent definition. Section 7031(c) of the State Department appropriations act bars foreign government officials and their immediate family members from US entry where the Secretary of State has credible information of "significant corruption" or "gross violation of human rights." The statute explicitly lists "corruption related to the extraction of natural resources" as illustrative. That is precisely the Zlochevsky and Kolomoyskyy fact pattern. But 7031(c) targets *officials* for *their* conduct. It does not address sovereign-source financial flows to connected businesses in another country. The two frameworks share an interest in foreign corruption but operate on different transactions: 7031(c) on the misuse of office, the foreign Emoluments Clause on the receipt of foreign-state-source value by domestic officeholders. The methodology piece should make this distinction explicit, because the natural-resources-extraction language in 7031(c) makes the framework look closer to this tracker's beat than it actually is.

## Disclosed gaps

Things this file does not establish on primary sources and where the methodology piece should not state without further verification:

- **The claim that Zlochevsky was sanctioned by US Treasury or State Department in January 2021 was not verified against the primary record.** A direct search of OFAC press releases and the State Department 7031(c) public designation archive did not surface a Zlochevsky designation. The contemporaneous designations that probably account for the secondary-source claim are: (a) the January 11, 2021 OFAC action against the Andrii Derkach Russian-influence network, which mentioned Burisma context but did not designate Zlochevsky, and (b) the March 5, 2021 7031(c) public designation of Ihor Kolomoyskyy and family for corruption as Dnipropetrovsk Oblast Governor. The methodology piece should state that the Burisma-adjacent figure who was publicly designated under 7031(c) is Kolomoyskyy (alleged true beneficial owner per AntAC, denied by Kolomoyskyy, not the documented owner of record), and should not state that Zlochevsky was sanctioned in 2021 absent a direct State or OFAC citation. If a privately imposed 7031(c) designation exists for Zlochevsky, the public record would not show it; the disclosed-gap principle applies.
- The exact compensation Hunter Biden personally received (as opposed to entity-level inflows to Rosemont Seneca Bohai). The Reuters bank-record figures are entity-level. Personal-level figures depend on the Hunter Biden tax case record and his book/deposition testimony, which I have not read in full.
- The contents of the House Ways and Means whistleblower email exhibits referenced in the November 2023 release. These are paraphrased in the committee memorandum but the underlying emails would need to be reviewed for accurate quotation.
- The full text and sealing status of the Senate HSGAC/Finance joint report's underlying Treasury suspicious activity reports. The September 2020 joint report describes them; the underlying SARs are not public.
- Whether AntAC's 2012 Kolomoyskyy-as-true-owner finding has been confirmed or contradicted by post-2014 forensic accounting. John Helmer's reporting argues yes; mainstream English-language coverage has generally treated Zlochevsky-via-Brociti as the operative ownership structure. The 2021 7031(c) designation of Kolomoyskyy does not adjudicate this question; it is grounded in his gubernatorial conduct, not in any Burisma ownership claim.
