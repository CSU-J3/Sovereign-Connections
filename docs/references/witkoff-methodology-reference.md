# Witkoff: Working Reference for the Sovereign-Adjacent Methodology Test

This file pulls out what bears on the central methodology question — does the Steve Witkoff fact pattern qualify under the tracker's foreign sovereign or sovereign-adjacent definition, and if not, what definitional change would be required to bring it in. Companion to the Burisma reference, written under the same disclosed-gap discipline. Not a comprehensive history of the Witkoff Group's deal book or Witkoff's diplomatic record. Functional reference for methodology calibration.

The Witkoff case is the inverse test of Burisma. Burisma is the case that fails the sovereign-adjacent rule despite political salience. Witkoff is the case that qualifies under the existing v1.x rule on the WLF side (through the Trump-family interest, with documented Abu Dhabi state-backed inflows) and qualifies more sparingly than the secondary reporting suggested on the Witkoff Group side once primary records are checked. The case also contains the documented sovereign-source-to-Trump-family flow the methodology is built around, which Burisma doesn't. Two findings shape the methodology read more than expected: the Apollo-channel flows that secondary reporting framed as "Apollo Trust, partly owned by Qatar" trace to a NYSE-listed REIT with QIA as a minority public shareholder and a wholly-owned Apollo insurance subsidiary, neither of which satisfies the controlling-interest-chain test; and the Pakistan / Roosevelt Hotel sequence is the cleanest single-fact-pattern qualifying record in the live tracker.

## Ownership and corporate structure

Two operating businesses are in scope here, with overlapping personnel and distinct ownership structures.

**Witkoff Group LLC.** Real estate firm founded 1997 by Steven C. Witkoff, headquartered in New York with offices in Los Angeles and Miami. Per the firm's spokesperson statements in September 2025 reporting, Steve Witkoff has had "no involvement" in operations since 2024 and is a minority shareholder. The firm's August 13, 2025 OGE Form 278e disclosure (Witkoff's New Entrant Report on file with the White House) shows continuing equity exposures across multiple Witkoff Group affiliates and pass-through vehicles, including Witkoff 44 LLC, 150 PH-B LLC, an interest in a captive law firm for the Witkoff Group and affiliates, and various SCW-numbered corporations. The form indicates Witkoff divested a $120 million interest in one Witkoff Group holding but does not specify which holding.

**World Liberty Financial Inc. (WLF).** Decentralized finance protocol and stablecoin issuer founded 2024 by Zachary Folkman, Chase Herro, Alex Witkoff, Zach Witkoff, and Trump family members (Donald Trump Jr., Eric Trump, Barron Trump). Donald Trump and Steve Witkoff are listed in corporate documentation as "co-founders emeritus." Trump family entities receive 75% of net proceeds from WLFI token sales and a cut of stablecoin profits, per Wikipedia's summary of corporate disclosures. The Witkoff family holds an unspecified stake and receives a cut of token sales. The WC Digital Fi LLC vehicle, identified in WLF documentation as "an affiliate of Steve Witkoff and certain of his family members," appears on Witkoff's August 2025 OGE form along with WC Digital SC LLC and SC Financial Technologies LLC.

The WLF capital structure changed materially on January 16, 2025, four days before Trump's second inauguration. A vehicle (Aryam) controlled by Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al Nahyan acquired a 49% stake in WLF for $500 million, with $187 million flowing to Trump family entities and $31 million flowing to Witkoff-associated entities at signing per Public Citizen's reading of the underlying documents reported by the Wall Street Journal in January 2026. The transaction was not publicly disclosed for over a year.

**Methodology read on ownership:** WLF is in scope under v1.x. Trump family interest is direct and documented, the company receives sovereign-adjacent foreign money (analyzed below), and the financial flow to family entities is documented in dollar terms. Witkoff Group requires v2.0 (the proposed covered-intermediary expansion) to come in scope. The minority-shareholder framing in company statements does not remove the documented financial interest visible on the OGE form; minority is not zero.

## The sovereign-adjacent ties that exist

Three counterparties carry the bulk of the sovereign-source flow. Each gets its own governance assessment because the SWFI per-fund documentation principle requires it.

**Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al Nahyan and the Abu Dhabi state-backed cluster.** Sheikh Tahnoon is the brother of UAE President Mohamed bin Zayed, the UAE's National Security Advisor, and chair of multiple state-linked investment vehicles including ADQ (one of Abu Dhabi's three sovereign wealth funds), the International Holding Company (IHC), G42 (AI/tech holding company), and MGX (AI-focused investment vehicle launched 2024). The House Select Committee on the CCP's February 4, 2026 letter to Zach Witkoff frames Sheikh Tahnoon's status with unusual clarity: he "serves at the pinnacle of the UAE's intelligence services and security apparatus" while simultaneously managing "Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund and multiple other technology-focused investment firms." The Public Citizen analysis notes that Tahnoon's investment portfolio is "difficult to draw a distinction" from his policy portfolio.

The vehicle that bought 49% of WLF in January 2025 was Aryam, identified in WSJ reporting as a Tahnoon lieutenants' vehicle. The MGX $2 billion investment in Binance, announced May 1, 2025 and structured to settle in WLF's USD1 stablecoin, was made through MGX directly. MGX is Abu Dhabi-state-backed per ABC News reporting and Wikipedia's WLF entry; Tahnoon chairs it.

This is the cleanest sovereign-source documentation in the file. Tahnoon's dual-hat role is the sovereign-adjacency question in its purest form: a senior foreign government official whose private investment vehicles cannot be cleanly separated from his official portfolio, deploying capital into a US presidential family's business while the US side simultaneously authorized advanced AI chip exports to UAE-linked companies (November 19, 2025) and conducted Iran negotiations in which the UAE has a stated policy interest.

**Qatar Investment Authority direct transactions.** QIA is Qatar's sovereign wealth fund, approximately $450 billion AUM, textbook sovereign per the SWFI list. QIA's August 2023 Park Lane Hotel transaction valued at $623 million is structurally more layered than headline coverage frames it. Per Steve Witkoff's own May 2025 statement at The Real Deal forum (surfaced in the September 26 NYT piece): the operating partnership was Witkoff and Mubadala. QIA held a mezzanine loan against the Mubadala equity position. QIA foreclosed on the mezzanine and took over the property at the entity level. The transaction predates Witkoff's envoy appointment by 16 months and belongs in a comparative pre-envoy section, not the live tracker. Both QIA (mezzanine holder turned owner) and Mubadala (foreclosed-out equity partner) were in the capital stack; both are sovereign, both pre-date the envoy role.

**Apollo-channel flows: documented but out of scope.** Initial secondary-source framing of "Apollo Trust, partly owned by Qatar" was traced to two distinct Apollo entities, both of which fail the controlling-interest-chain test. The framing also carried an imprecision the NYT itself subsequently corrected.

The Brook (52-story Brooklyn rental tower, 591 units, partnership with Witkoff): financed by Apollo Commercial Real Estate Finance Inc. (NYSE: ARI), a publicly traded mortgage REIT managed by an Apollo Global Management affiliate. ARI took control of the development site through foreclosure on prior developer RedSky in August 2022, with Witkoff joining the project. QIA has been an ARI common shareholder since 2015. Per the Witkoff Group's Parlatore Law Group letter to NYT (October 2, 2025), QIA holds approximately $125 million common equity and $160 million preferred — under 10% of ARI's $1.5 billion equity cap, with no board seat, no approval authority, and no decision-making rights. Per Middle East Forum (April 2023 data), QIA was the third-largest ARI common shareholder behind BlackRock and Vanguard at 7.4%. Apollo spokeswoman Joanna Rose told NYT that investors like Qatar in the company's real estate trust "have no role in directing or choosing the investments" the trust makes, and that investments in Witkoff properties represented a small percentage of the trust's overall deals. QIA spokeswoman Melanie Dunn told NYT that QIA "was not invested in the Brook or Belgrove" but did not answer questions about investments being made via the Apollo trust.

Belgrove Palm Beach ($100 million loan, May 2025): the original NYT piece initially framed this as coming from "the Apollo trust, a fund partly owned by Qatar." NYT published a formal correction on October 5, 2025: "An earlier version of this article referred imprecisely to the source of $100 million received by the Witkoff Group for the Belgrove property. The lender in that transaction was Apollo Global Management, a private-equity firm that has worked with Qatar, not a financial trust that is a subsidiary of Apollo and partly owned by Qatar." Apollo Global Management is publicly traded (NYSE: APO) with Vanguard, BlackRock, State Street, and Capital Group as principal shareholders. No documented QIA stake in APO at any control-implying level. Athene, Apollo's annuity subsidiary, merged into APO as a wholly-owned subsidiary on January 1, 2022.

Neither flow satisfies the v2.0 controlling-interest-chain test. A minority public-market shareholder with no board seat, no approval authority, and no decision-making rights does not constitute sovereign control over a NYSE-listed REIT's lending decisions. A NYSE-listed asset manager's loan from corporate balance sheet is not a sovereign-source vehicle absent a documented controlling stake at the parent. Both Apollo flows are out of scope, and the NYT's own correction confirms the underlying framing was imprecise.

The contested narrative-level claim from Joey Allaham (former Stonington Strategies lobbyist for Qatar) that in early 2018 Sheikh Mohammed bin Hamad al-Thani proposed at QIA's Manhattan headquarters using Apollo as a vehicle to invest in Trump-allies' projects is documented in the NYT piece. The strategic framing was that QIA was the third-largest shareholder in Apollo's publicly traded real estate financing trust (ARI), and could be steered toward Trump-allied real estate without QIA itself directing the trust's investment decisions. Apollo and QIA both deny that this strategy was implemented; Allaham fell out with the Qatari government later in 2018 and is dismissed by the White House as a Democrat-affiliated source. The Allaham material does not change the structural test (the controlling-interest-chain finding stands), but it is documented context for the relationship's articulated strategic intent. The same finding would attach to a Democratic-side analogue under the symmetry test: 7.4% public-market shareholding does not turn the issuer's loans into sovereign payments, regardless of what Allaham or anyone else articulates as the relationship's purpose.

**Mubadala Investment Company.** Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund (~$330 billion AUM, SWFI-listed). Held a stake in the Park Lane Hotel partnership during the period preceding the 2023 QIA exit transaction. The Mubadala stake was acquired during the Justice Department's recovery effort related to the Jho Low / 1MDB scandal — the 2017 forfeiture chain that pulled Low's beneficial interest out of the property. Mubadala is sovereign-source per the SWFI list. The Park Lane stake predates Witkoff's envoy appointment by years and is comparative-set rather than live-tracker material.

**Lower-priority but documented:** Alex Witkoff's pitched "Special Situations Real Estate Credit Fund" sought $4 billion from QIA, the UAE sovereign funds, and Kuwait Investment Authority during 2024 and into 2025. QIA confirmed it was approached and declined. The fund did not close, per Witkoff Group's September 2025 statement. Reuters and NYT reporting indicate Alex Witkoff continued representing pledged commitments to other potential investors as recently as August 2025. No closed transaction. Worth a comparative-set entry as "attempted sovereign-adjacent inflow, did not close" rather than a record in the live tracker.

**The Pakistan / Roosevelt Hotel transaction.** January 2026: Zach Witkoff negotiated an agreement on Pakistan's behalf to incorporate WLF cryptocurrency into Pakistan's financial system, with Pakistan's finance minister Muhammad Aurangzeb. Steve Witkoff subsequently negotiated, in his envoy capacity, a US-Pakistan exploration of the redevelopment of the Roosevelt Hotel in Manhattan, which the Pakistani government owns. This sequence — Witkoff family business deal followed by Witkoff government deal involving the same counterparty and an asset owned by that foreign government — is the cleanest single-fact-pattern illustration of the sovereign-adjacent-to-connected-business question the tracker is built around. The Roosevelt is owned by Pakistan International Airlines Investment Limited, a Pakistani SOE. This belongs in the live tracker.

## The sovereign-adjacent ties that don't exist

The disclosure principle from the Burisma file applies in reverse here. To be explicit about what the documented record does and does not establish:

- **Saudi PIF direct investment in Witkoff Group or WLF is not documented in the public record I've reviewed.** Witkoff appeared at the Future Investment Initiative Institute event in Miami in February 2025 alongside Kushner, but appearance is not investment. PIF money is the central case in the Affinity Partners record (Kushner) — it is not, on present evidence, a Witkoff record.
- The 2023 QIA Park Lane purchase predates Witkoff's envoy role by 16 months. It belongs in a comparative pre-envoy section, not the live tracker.
- The Justin Sun / WLF / dropped SEC investigation sequence is documented but does not involve foreign sovereign money. Sun is a private actor. This belongs in a different oversight beat (SEC enforcement, presidential pardon power) and is referenced here only because it overlaps the WLF entity.
- Witkoff Group's standard real estate credit transactions with non-sovereign capital sources (private equity, US bank financing) are out of scope by definition, no matter the dollar size.
- The Russia-Ukraine negotiations and the WSJ-reported communications-security questions are out of scope. Different beat (foreign-influence on US negotiating posture). Not sovereign-source-to-connected-business.

## Witkoff envoy engagement and policy portfolio

**Appointments and titles.** Trump announced Witkoff as Special Envoy to the Middle East on November 12, 2024. Witkoff began functioning in the role in December 2024 (joining Brett McGurk on the Hamas hostage-deal negotiations) and was formally in office at the January 20, 2025 inauguration. On June 30, 2025, Witkoff was appointed Assistant to the President and Special Envoy for Peace Missions, broadening the portfolio. His public title as of the OGE form is the latter; his X bio reads "Ambassador at Large and Special Envoy to the Middle East." He did not go through Senate confirmation.

**Portfolio overlap.** This is the methodology-relevant question for the v2.0 rule. Witkoff's portfolio has covered:
- Israel-Hamas ceasefire and hostage negotiations (Qatar as central mediator; Egypt; Saudi Arabia and UAE as regional alignment partners)
- US-Iran nuclear talks (Oman as venue; UAE as policy stakeholder; Pakistan as recent venue)
- Russia-Ukraine negotiations (Riyadh as Saudi-hosted venue; the Saudi government as facilitator)
- US Sovereign Wealth Fund executive order signing (February 3, 2025), where Trump publicly gestured to Witkoff
- AI chip export decisions toward UAE-linked entities (Witkoff's role in the policy sequence is the question Senators Warren and Murphy have asked OGE about; not yet established by primary record)

The portfolio-overlap test under the proposed v2.0 rule is satisfied for every counterparty discussed in the prior section. A Middle East envoy receiving (directly or through family or business interest) sovereign-source money from Gulf governments while negotiating with those same governments is the structural pattern v2.0 is designed to capture.

**Compensation status.** Witkoff does not draw a federal salary and pays his own travel per White House framing. The financial-interest question is not whether he is government-paid; it is whether his retained business interests received sovereign-source money during his official tenure. The OGE form establishes both the retained interests and (by absence) the unresolved divestment status.

## The convergent-interest question

This is the section the Burisma file does not have an analogue for, because Burisma's payments did not flow back to the principal. Here they do.

The Trump family take from WLF, layered out:
- 75% of net proceeds from WLFI token sales, per Wikipedia citing corporate documentation
- A cut of stablecoin profits
- $187 million up-front from the Tahnoon Aryam transaction in January 2025
- An estimated $1 billion in profits as of December 2025 with $3 billion of unsold tokens still held, per Wikipedia citing reporting
- An estimated $5 billion windfall when WLFI token trading opened, per ABC News earlier reporting

These figures vary across sources because they describe different things (realized vs. unrealized, family vs. entity-level, gross vs. net). The variance pattern matches the Burisma compensation-figure variance — entity-level inflow, principal-level take, total-pool figures all get conflated in secondary reporting. For tracker purposes, the documented dollar flow into Trump family entities at the Tahnoon deal closing ($187 million) is the cleanest single number to anchor on; everything else is corroborating context.

The Witkoff family take from WLF at the same transaction was $31 million per Public Citizen's reading of WSJ documents. Plus an unspecified continuing stake.

Public Citizen and the House Select Committee on the CCP have both invoked the foreign Emoluments Clause framework explicitly. This places the WLF record in the tracker's central case category — sovereign-adjacent foreign source paying value to a business with documented financial interest of a named family member of the current administration — with the additional documented feature that the same transaction also paid a senior administration official (Witkoff) personally.

This is the pattern the user's "looping Trump in on a cut" framing describes. The structural rule still has to be definable without depending on documenting the kickback in every case, because in most appointee cases the kickback won't be on a public record. But where it is documented — as in WLF — the convergent-interest flag should attach and the record should sit in a higher evidence category than appointee-only intermediary records.

## Investigations and ethics-process status

The investigations record on the Witkoff fact pattern is short, recent, and largely ongoing. Distinct from Burisma's long-and-inconclusive investigations record.

- **Senate Banking, Foreign Relations, and ethics-related letters.** Senators Schiff, Slotkin, Booker, Durbin, and five others sent Witkoff a letter on October 22, 2025 asking why he had not divested his WLF stake despite May 2025 representations from WLF that he was "fully divesting." Senators Warren and Murphy followed with a November 17-18, 2025 letter to the White House and OGE asking why Witkoff's August 13, 2025 OGE Form 278e had not been signed off by an ethics official more than 60 days after the statutory review deadline.
- **House Select Committee on the Strategic Competition Between the United States and the Chinese Communist Party.** Ranking Member sent a February 4, 2026 letter to Zach Witkoff at WLF requesting documents and information about the Tahnoon transaction and any other UAE or PRC-based agreements with WLF. Cited Reuters, the WSJ, and White House gallery records.
- **No public DOJ or OFAC action against Witkoff, the Witkoff Group, or WLF as of this writing.** No FARA filings related to WLF's foreign-government counterparty discussions are documented in the public record I've reviewed.
- **No agency ethics-officer sign-off on Witkoff's financial disclosure** as of the Warren/Murphy letter date. The 60-day statutory review deadline passed in October 2025.

The ethics-process status itself is methodology-relevant. An OGE Form 278e is a primary record. A 278e that has been filed but not signed off by the agency ethics officer establishes (a) the financial interests as filer-disclosed, and (b) the unresolved compliance status as agency-process-disclosed. Both belong in the record.

## Methodology assessment

Walking the v1.x sovereign-and-sovereign-adjacent definition through the WLF subset of the Witkoff fact pattern:

| Criterion | WLF |
|---|---|
| Direct foreign government payment | Mixed (Tahnoon vehicle is private but state-controlled; MGX is state-backed) |
| Sovereign wealth fund per SWFI list | Indirectly (MGX is associated with ADQ governance cluster; not listed in its own right) |
| State-owned enterprise | Yes for MGX-as-Abu-Dhabi-state-backed |
| Controlling-interest chain to sovereign | Yes (Tahnoon's official roles + state-backed firm chair status) |
| Foreign government official acting in official capacity | Yes (Tahnoon as UAE NSA / SWF chair) |
| Named family member with financial interest | Yes (Trump children co-founders, Trump emeritus, 75% of net token proceeds to family) |

**WLF qualifies under v1.x.** The Tahnoon-Aryam-WLF transaction and the MGX-USD1-Binance transaction are tracker records.

Walking the same definition through the Witkoff Group subset, transaction by transaction:

| Transaction | Sovereign source | Family member with interest | v1.x verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| QIA Park Lane purchase, Aug 2023 | Yes (QIA, direct) | No | Out of scope (no family interest); comparative pre-envoy |
| Mubadala mezzanine in Park Lane chain | Yes (Mubadala, direct) | No | Out of scope; comparative pre-envoy |
| ARI loan to The Brook, 2022 onward | No (ARI is NYSE REIT; QIA <10% with no control rights) | No | Out of scope |
| Athene Belgrove loan, May 2025 | No (Athene is wholly-owned APO subsidiary) | No | Out of scope |
| Special Situations Real Estate Credit Fund pitch, 2024–25 | Would have been (QIA, KIA, UAE SWFs) | No | Did not close; comparative-set candidate |

**No Witkoff Group transactions qualify under v1.x.** Fails on the family-member criterion in every case, and the Apollo flows additionally fail on the sovereign-source criterion.

Walking the proposed v2.0 covered-intermediary rule against the same transactions:

| Transaction | Appointee with interest | Sovereign-source counterparty | Portfolio overlap | v2.0 verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| QIA Park Lane purchase, Aug 2023 | Yes (Witkoff retained interest) | Yes (QIA) | Yes (ME envoy / Gulf state) | In scope but pre-envoy timing → comparative |
| Mubadala mezzanine in Park Lane chain | Yes | Yes (Mubadala) | Yes | Comparative pre-envoy |
| ARI loan to The Brook | Yes | No | Yes | Out of scope (sovereign-source fails) |
| Athene Belgrove loan, May 2025 | Yes | No | Yes | Out of scope (sovereign-source fails) |
| Roosevelt Hotel exploration, Jan 2026 | Yes | Yes (Pakistan via PIA Investment Ltd.) | Yes | In scope, live tracker |

**One Witkoff Group transaction qualifies under v2.0 in the live tracker: the Roosevelt Hotel exploration, on the appointee side of a deal Steve Witkoff negotiated personally as envoy.** The QIA and Mubadala Park Lane transactions are comparative-set entries because they predate the envoy appointment. The Apollo flows fall out under either rule version. The pitched-but-not-closed $4 billion Special Situations fund belongs in a comparative-attempted-inflow section.

WLF retains both v1.x records (Tahnoon-Aryam, MGX-USD1-Binance) with the convergent-interest flag attached because of the documented dual flow at the Tahnoon transaction. The Pakistan WLF crypto agreement (Zach Witkoff, January 2026) is an additional v1.x candidate record on the WLF side, paired with the Roosevelt deal on the Witkoff Group side around the same Pakistani counterparty.

The right framing for the methodology piece: Witkoff is the case the v2.0 expansion is built to capture, but the expansion captures fewer Witkoff Group transactions than the secondary reporting suggested. The Apollo finding is the worked example for why the controlling-interest-chain test exists in the methodology — to keep public-market minority shareholdings from being treated as sovereign control. Symmetrically applied: an opposite-party envoy with the same Apollo-channel financing structure would also fall out under the same rule. WLF and the Pakistan/Roosevelt sequence are the records doing the methodological work for v2.0; Apollo is the record demonstrating the rule's bounds.

## Disclosed gaps

Things this file does not establish on primary sources and where the methodology piece should not state without further verification:

- **Apollo-channel sovereign-source question (closed against in-scope; preserved here for the methodology audit trail).** Initial secondary-source framing of "Apollo Trust, partly owned by Qatar" was traced to two distinct Apollo entities, both of which fail the controlling-interest-chain test. ARI is a NYSE-listed mortgage REIT with QIA as a <10% public-market common-and-preferred shareholder since 2015 with no board seat or approval rights. Athene is a wholly-owned subsidiary of NYSE-listed APO with no documented QIA controlling stake. Verified against: Apollo's January 3, 2022 merger close announcement, Athora's organisation page citing the Apollo merger, Witkoff Group's Parlatore Law Group letter to NYT (October 2, 2025), and Middle East Forum's April 2023 ARI shareholding data. Remaining sub-question worth noting but not blocking publication: whether QIA's ARI stake has changed materially after April 2023; current 13G/F filings on ARI would resolve.
- **The specific Witkoff Group holding from which $120 million was divested.** The OGE Form 278e references the divestment without identifying the holding. Without identification, it is not possible to determine whether the divested holding was a sovereign-counterparty exposure or a domestic asset.
- **Witkoff's actual divestment status from WLF as of the most recent date.** Senators Schiff and Slotkin's October 22, 2025 letter establishes that as of August 13, 2025, Witkoff still held WLF cryptocurrency, WC Digital Fi LLC, WC Digital SC LLC, and SC Financial Technologies LLC. Whether these have since been divested is not in the primary record I've reviewed.
- **The size of the Witkoff family stake in WLF.** Reporting says "unspecified" for both the holding entity vehicle and the percentage. The $31 million Tahnoon deal allocation establishes a floor, not the stake size.
- **Whether the AI chip export approvals to UAE-linked companies in November 2025 were preceded by Witkoff participation in the deliberations.** The Senate ethics letters identify this as the open question; the answer depends on records the OGE process has not surfaced.
- **The Roosevelt Hotel deal status.** Wikipedia references the Steve Witkoff / Aurangzeb negotiation as having occurred; whether a definitive agreement, MOU, or merely an exploratory framework exists is not yet clear from secondary reporting. Pakistan's State Department of Privatisation records and the relevant US government interlocutor (likely State Department or Commerce) would be the primary sources.
- **The exact valuation of the Witkoff Group's "minority shareholder" status post-2024 reorganization.** The September 2025 spokesperson statements assert no operational involvement and minority-shareholder status. The OGE form establishes equity exposure but not aggregate percentage. Cap-table-level documentation would resolve this; it is not public.
- **Whether any FARA filings exist for any Witkoff Group, WLF, or Witkoff-family-affiliated lobbying or representation activity related to Qatar, the UAE, Kuwait, or other Gulf-state counterparties.** A FARA Quick Search for "Witkoff" returned no results in my prior pulls but I have not done an exhaustive run for related entity names; this should be checked before publication.
